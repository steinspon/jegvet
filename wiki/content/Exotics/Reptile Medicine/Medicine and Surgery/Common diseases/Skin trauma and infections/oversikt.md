# Skin trauma and infections

Trauma can be from other household pets, conspecifics, prey items (livefood) or due to burns from inappropriate heat sources such as mats or hot rocks. Substantial damage and pain can occur. Secondary infections with bacterial or fungal agents are commonly seen in traumatised areas. These can occur as primary conditions where husbandry is poor and the animal is in squalid conditions. As reptiles shed their skin regularly the healing capacity is huge and substantial wounds can heal providing secondary complications are suitable controlled. Do not be scared to treat wounds as you would in a dog or cat. Wounds require aggressive debridement and flushing. Cultures can be taken and biopsy may be indicated. Primary closure should only be attempted if there is no infection evident, otherwise wounds should be treated as open wounds and allow healing by secondary intention. Underlying damage to the body wall and bony elements is possible. Punctures into the coelomic cavity are also highly likely. A thorough examination under anaesthesia is warranted and radiography may be required for a complete evaluation. Standard dressings and products used for dogs and cats are appropriate based on the wound type. Silver based products have a high level of activity against gram negatives (including Pseudomonas sp) and fungi which can be common opportunistic pathogens in reptiles. Treatment involves fluid therapy, analgesics, antibiotics and surgical therapy.

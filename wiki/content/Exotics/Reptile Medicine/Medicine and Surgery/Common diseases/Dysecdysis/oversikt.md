# Dysecdysis

Low relative humidity can lead to dysecdysis, respiratory disease, skin disease or renal disease over time. Ecdysis is the term used when a squamate sheds it skin. This is an important aspect of growth of the reptile. A second layer of skin is produced from the basal layer and lymph enters the cleavage zone to separate the old skin from the new. At this stage snakes and lizards may go a dull colour and because the snakes have skin over their eyes (the spectacles) then this lymph imparts a blue colour to the eyes. This is known as being ‘in the blue’ and lasts for three days. After this time the lymph clears and three days later the snake sheds it skin. Snakes shed in one go and checking to ensure the spectacles have come away is important. Lizards shed piecemeal and often will eaten the exuvium (waste not want not). Retained shed can lead to constriction of the digits and tail and obscure vision. Snakes and lizards can suffer from dyspnoea if it obstructs the nares. Dysecdysis occurs when the skin is not shed and can be due to lack of humidity as already explained or due to skin infections, parasite, trauma or general debility. Treatment involves addressing the husbandry and using the techniques mention to elevate humidity. Bathing and topical ocular lubricants can be used to soften retained spectacles. Anaesthesia and gentle manipulation of retained spectacles or skin with forceps may be required to prevent loss of digits or vision.
